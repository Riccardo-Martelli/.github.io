Riccardo's Portfolio Notes

You're standing in front of a room, explaining something you thought you knew well. A student raises their hand with a sharp question. You pause. In that pause you realise you don't actually have a complete answer. Part disappointment in yourself, part thrill. You tell them you'll come back with a proper answer once you've thought it through. And you do, because the question forced you to actually learn the thing you were supposedly teaching.

That happened to me a hundred times over, with students from Europe to the UK and US. It taught me something: the illusion of understanding is more dangerous than admitting what you don't know. We think we've mastered a subject. Usually we haven't. Teaching exposes that fast.

Most people mistake familiarity for mastery. You've read the papers, done the problem sets, taught the concept a dozen times, so you assume you understand it completely. But understanding isn't binary. There are depths you haven't reached, connections you haven't made, gaps hiding in the language you use to explain it. Saying something out loud to another person is a different act from thinking it. The soft spots show up immediately. A question exposes them. A confused look forces you to rephrase, to dig into why something is true instead of just asserting that it is. That discomfort isn't failure. It's the moment learning actually starts.

Most people stop right there. They feel the unease and assume they've failed, that a good teacher should have every answer ready. It's backwards. That discomfort is the signal you've hit the edge of your own understanding, and now you get to cross it. The best teaching moments I've had weren't the ones where I knew everything. They were the ones where a student asked something that made me say "I don't know, but I'm going to find out," and then I actually had to: chase it through papers, through conversations, until I understood it properly.

Teaching doesn't stop at the classroom door. Share ideas publicly, on a blog, on LinkedIn, in conversation, and you invite the same cycle from strangers. Someone reads what you wrote, spots a gap, asks a question. Or they're holding a piece of the puzzle you're missing. I've ended up co-writing papers with people I met exactly that way, through sharing research I found interesting and just talking about it. None of that happens if you keep the work private. A conversation gets you further than solitary work ever does.

Clarity does something else, too. It filters. Explain something honestly, without hiding behind unnecessary complexity, and you reveal who you actually are. A weak character needs the mask, needs obscurity as a defence against criticism. A strong one can take the criticism and keep sharing anyway.

The same test happens even without a student in the room. When I read a paper or a textbook, I keep a ruler and a pencil next to it and take notes as I go. Most of the time a concept doesn't stick on the first pass. I let it sit for a minute, then I try to rebuild it myself with a simple problem. Can I reproduce the result? Can I push it one step further and get something new? If not, that gap is the same gap a student's question would have found. The theory on the page looks clear until you try to reproduce it yourself.

Coding works the same way, just faster. You can follow the logic of a language and still not be able to build anything with it. The only way through is to write code, watch it break, and debug it. Every bug you chase teaches you something the documentation didn't. Most of the small projects I build this way don't produce anything new. What they leave behind is a skill I didn't have before I started.

This is what I think we're quietly losing with artificial intelligence (AI). Getting stuck used to be the point, not an inconvenience to route around. You'd bump your head against a problem for an hour, get nowhere, and that frustration was doing something: forcing you to actually understand what you were missing. Ask an AI model for the answer and the discomfort disappears, but so does the reason it was there. It's the same mechanism as teaching, just with no one else in the room to ask you the hard question. If nobody else will make you sit with not knowing, you have to make yourself do it.

I learned that clarity and simplicity in exposition are a key factor in challenging your own understanding and therefore producing growth by moving from physics into finance. Around two in the morning, talking to someone finishing a master's degree in finance, I mentioned I wanted to move into the field myself. He looked at me and said: "You want to go into finance? What's the difference between a stock and a bond?" Not a real question. A test, meant to make the outsider feel small. And the irony is that it isn't even a hard question. Finance isn't closed off by nature, no field is. A field of study gets artificially gatekept by people who need you to feel like you don't belong so they can feel like they do. It is easier to toss a smoke screen of complexity or uncleanness masked as knowledge rather than admit the limits of their own understanding, this is a recipe to failure and a clear sign of lacking the roots for growth.

Vladimir Arnold called out the same instinct in mathematics. In his essay "On Teaching Mathematics", he argued that algebraists define a group through a pile of forgettable axioms nobody could reasonably absorb on first contact, and that mathematicians often push students through unmotivated, needlessly hard proofs partly to raise the standing of the subject, and of the people teaching it.

The obstacle was never the subject. It was people hiding behind a mask of difficulty. Some of the strongest quant funds, Jim Simons' Renaissance and Jean-Philippe Bouchaud's Capital Fund Management among them, hire physicists and mathematicians precisely because they think clearly and attack a problem directly. No mask required.

That's why I share this the way I do: no paywall, no gatekeeping. Clarity invites better questions. Better questions strengthen the work. And the people it attracts are the ones strong enough to engage honestly instead of defending territory.
